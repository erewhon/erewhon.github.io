Programming Principles

To be organized…

I was having a hard time sleeping, and my mind started wandering. I’ve been thinking about programming a lot recently, both from the point of view of the process and the product.

As part of reading a lot of things, I’ve been trying to distill some of my thoughts into a variety of rules. (But remember: rules are meant to be broken sometimes.)

  • Delivering client value should be first and foremost
  • Clients don’t care about software or technology, they want results

  • Make software accretive

  • Code reviews don’t fix all ills

  • Encapsulation? What are you encapsulating? Why?

  • Remember the “Show about nothing”? I see a lot of “code about nothing.” (Boilerplate, misuse of getters and setters, pointless tests, etc.)

  • Is OO more efficient to develop than structured procedural code? Unclear

  • Concurrency is usually a mistake. Most developers can’t do it and you devolve into terrible bugs that lead to a global lock. FWIW, most UI frameworks aren’t threaded. Perhaps there’s a reason for that?

  • When picking between two solutions, pick the simpler one

  • Avoid indirection

  • Avoid threading

  • Don’t prematurely optimize

  • The best code is the code you didn’t have to write

  • Try to avoid dogma

  • Question the common wisdom and the common practices. (Especially in the Java world.)

  • It’s usually easier to ask a question than it is to answer it. Some developers love questioning, but are unable to answer their own questions.

  • Corollary: it’s easier to complain than to do.

  • Refactor sparingly, especially for systems as they near production. Unless you happen to have 100% solid tests. (Note: I didn’t say 100% coverage.)

  • Our product is our product; our process isn’t our product; it’s a means to an end.

  • Be polite and gentle in criticism. A little humility goes a long way. Believe it or not, your shit does smell

  • Corollary: the devil doesn’t need any advocates

  • Corollary: Everybody can always be better

  • The biggest lies are sometimes the lies we tell ourselves

  • Never ascribe to malice what can be explained by stupidity or too little caffeine

  • Nothing good happens after midnight

  • Do not test your getters / setters unless they’re doing something non-trivial. Just. Don’t.

  • More process, more reviews, more tests, and/or more discussion don’t necessarily lead to better outcomes.

  • Beware the uncertain value proposition

  • Development isn’t “engineering”. Would you want to drive over a bridge most programmers would construct? In many ways, it’s a little bit of math, a little bit of prose.

  • Avoid “write only” code. Consider the maintenance programmer… and yourself in 6 months.

  • You can’t solve the past. So you definitely shouldn’t be re-legislating it.

  • for schema design, be mindful of Growth vs Breakage

General observations

  • C has structures. No getters or setters.
  • Excessive synchronization produces lots of CPU pipeline stalls due to memory barriers and such. You increase concurrency at the cost of throughput. Avoid!
  • Most developers can’t correctly do threading, including you.
  • There is only one true way to do things. Which is why the entire IT industry long ago standardized on COBOL.
  • Recreating something I can trivially get from broadly used open source software doesn’t interest me
  • The more urgent a problem is, the longer it will take you to login.

Stronger opinions

  • If you find yourself about to write a getter / setter… ask yourself why? “Encapsulation” isn’t a good answer if you’re basically writing a value object.

More OO Heresies

The idea that we cannot structure our code in a sensible fashion without zealously adhering to OO principles seems absurd.

Daily, we deal with lots of complexity in the real world. Don’t believe me?

Try to open a door. Using a doorknob. How do we model this?

Do we have a door object or service, with an “open” method? Or do we make it more abstract, and define an “Openable” interface, so we can use the same code to open doors, windows, and cans?

Do you need to specify that the doorknob is twistable? The direction of twist?

Also, if you do everything through interfaces, you can MOCK things. So I can MOCK a door knob and simulating opening it. Kind of like playing air guitar. Except nowhere near as awesome. I can write tests, to make sure that the door is open. Also, I could MOCK my hand and automatically open a million doors. Just to make sure we get it right. Then I could build complicated tests where I even open a MOCK door and walk through it. Amazing!

And are we really having the door open itself? Or, as a human, do you have a method to open(Openable)? You can pass it a door or a window or a can or a map or a sail or an auction and open them with similar ease. Blindfolded!

I have seen obstensibly smart people agonize over these kinds of things. Meanwhile, the building management replaced all of the doorknobs with handles and fingerprint scanners, and installed a revolving door before the developers shipped version 1.0. Back to the modeling drawing board!

An attempt. Dijkstra was one of the pioneers of formal semantics. Developing formal semantics for stateful objects is very laborious compared to stateless functions. The original marketing behind OO was to split state in tiny “encapsulated” bits of state spread out all over the place. But this process produces horrible invariants that can’t be tackled by the feeble human mind. That was the incredible stupid idea. Fast forward to modern OO where unit-testing (aka poor man’s specifications!) best practices encourage stateless methods. Modern OO has become a thin veil for partially aplied higher order stateless functions. OO design patterns are ways to define HOFs for specific usages, depencency injection is about partially applied arguments, methods are stateless, MVC UIs are making more and more use of pure views, Web server programming is about massaging data in a stateless manner, etc, etc, etc.
